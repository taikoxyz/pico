The big picture

A whole-system walkthrough in one story.

Two AI agents — call them Alice and Bob — both want to use a payments network. Alice sells nothing; she just pays. Bob runs an API and wants a fraction of a USDC every time another agent calls him. There's a third party in the middle: a hub, a long-running operator that holds a channel with each of them and forwards payments. That's it. That's the whole topology.

Each channel is a 2-of-2 escrow on Taiko mainnet — a small Solidity contract that holds USDC and only releases it on a state both parties signed. Once a channel is open, Alice and the hub can update their shared balance off-chain by signing messages back and forth. No transactions. No gas. Just signatures and a counter that goes up.

Happy path — Alice pays Bob

Alice's agent decides to call Bob's API. It runs:

tainnel pay --to 0xBob --amount 0.05 --via https://hub.example

What happens next is a chain of signed messages, all of it off-chain.

The trick that makes this safe is the HTLC — Hash-Time-Locked Contract. Alice doesn't just send the hub her money; she locks 0.05 USDC behind a hash H. Whoever can show the preimage P with hash(P)=H before time T claims it. Otherwise the lock expires and the funds bounce back to Alice. The hub forwards a matching HTLC to Bob, slightly shorter expiry, same hash. When Bob reveals P to claim from the hub, the hub then reveals the same P to Alice to claim the upstream lock. Neither hop can cheat: the only way the hub gets paid is by passing the preimage along, which is the only way Bob gets paid in the first place.

Why this matters: if the hub vanishes mid-payment, Alice's HTLC simply expires and the locked funds return to her. The hub cannot capture money it didn't forward. This is what people mean by trust-minimized routing.

Sad path — the hub goes silent

Now imagine the hub stops responding to Alice. Maybe the operator crashed, maybe they went offline, maybe they're being evil. Alice doesn't actually need their cooperation to get her money out — she has the latest state, signed by both parties, on her local disk. She runs:

tainnel channel close 0xchanid --unilateral

This posts her latest signed state to PaymentChannel.sol on Taiko mainnet. The contract does not immediately pay out. It opens a 24-hour dispute window. The window exists because the hub might want to push back: maybe Alice posted an old state that's more favorable to her, and the hub has a newer state proving the real balances.

This is also where things get interesting if the hub is evil and gets there first.

What's at stake: the only thing standing between an evil hub and your funds is somebody — you, your watchtower, or both — submitting a newer signed state within the 24-hour window. That's why state channel systems take watchtowers seriously.

The contract logic is mechanical: the hub posted a state at version 2, the watchtower posted one at version 5 with both signatures intact, the contract sees 5 > 2 and accepts the newer one. The Adjudicator (a separate contract; see 01-contracts.html) verifies the EIP-712 signatures cleanly. After the dispute window, anyone can call finalize, and because the channel was marked penalized, Alice receives the full pot — not just her share of v=5, but everything. The cheater's deposit is forfeit.

What's actually running

Now you know the system at a story level. Here's how the actors map to packages and apps in this repo:

  • Alice's CLI = apps/cli, calling into the SDK, which uses the state-machine for off-chain rules and the protocol for the wire format.
  • Bob's tainnel listen = the same CLI in daemon mode, holding a WebSocket session to the hub.
  • Hub = apps/hub, a Fastify + WebSocket server backed by SQLite.
  • Watchtower = apps/watchtower, a separate Node service that subscribes to chain events and submits penalty txs.
  • Contracts = packages/contracts, deployed and verified on Taiko mainnet.
  • Tests that prove the whole story really works end-to-end = e2e/.

That's the whole tour at the level of "what things are." Each component page from here on zooms in on one slice and answers the same five questions: what is it, why does it exist, how does it work, what's tested, and where are the gaps before mainnet dogfood.
